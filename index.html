INDIA · MAY 2026 · WHAT'S REALLY HAPPENING

India isn't asking. It's advising worried.

On 10 May 2026, PM Modi told citizens to skip buying gold, take public transport instead of cars, use less cooking oil, and avoid foreign trips. Two days later, the government raised the gold import duty to 15%. Then, on 13 May, the rupee crashed past 95 against the dollar — a record low. These weren't random lifestyle tips. They were warning signs from a government that knows something the public hasn't fully realized yet: India is quietly trying to protect its dollars before things get worse.

Reading: HIGH STRESS — but not a crisis yet. The pain is in two places: expensive oil and a weakening rupee. India still has $602 billion in foreign currency saved up, which is a thick safety cushion. But the government is acting before the problem gets worse — and that itself tells you something.
In one line
What's the problem?
India buys oil and gold from abroad using US dollars. When the world wants more dollars (or when India needs more), India's currency — the rupee — gets weaker. A weaker rupee makes everything we import more expensive: fuel, food, electronics, medicines.
In one line
Why is PM Modi asking us to cut back?
Every gold chain, every foreign trip, every litre of petrol you buy means India sends dollars abroad. If millions of people cut back even a little, India sends fewer dollars out — which slows down how fast the rupee is falling and how fast prices are rising.
In one line
Should I be worried?
Not panicked, but alert. India is in a "defensive" phase — like a country tightening its belt before things get rough. Petrol, food, and gold are likely to stay expensive for months. EMIs probably won't fall soon. Foreign travel and imported goods will pinch more.

— 09 / THE WORLD MAP

A blocked Red Sea is an Indian inflation problem.

India buys 85% of its oil from abroad. Most of it travels through two narrow stretches of water. If anything goes wrong there, prices in your local market change within weeks.

RED LINE · RED SEA RISK 8.4
The Suez Shortcut Is Blocked

About 12% of all global trade — and a big chunk of India's oil, electronics and fertilizer — passes through this stretch. With attacks happening here, ships are taking the long way around Africa. That adds 10–14 days to delivery and pushes up shipping costs, which shows up in our shops 2–3 months later.

WATCH · HORMUZ STRAIT RISK 7.8
One-Fifth of the World's Oil Squeezes Through Here

If this narrow strip of water between Iran and Oman ever closes (even briefly), oil could shoot to $130–150 a barrel. For India, that would mean petrol crossing ₹130/litre and the government having to make some tough emergency choices.

STRUCTURAL · TWO BIG EXPOSURES RISK 6.5
India's Twin Weakness — Oil and Gold

India imports about 85% of its oil, and is the world's second-largest gold buyer. Most of the time only one of these creates trouble. Right now, both are pulling against the rupee at the same time — which is what makes May 2026 different.

QUIET RISK · ELECTRONICS RISK 5.4
Phone, Laptop, and Chip Delays

The same Suez problem matters for time-sensitive electronics. A 4–6 week supply lag shows up as phone shortages at year-end, capex deferrals in IT services, and higher prices on imported electronics.

— 11 / THE BOTTOM LINE

What India is actually trying to do.

OUR CONFIDENCE · MEDIUM-HIGH
FOR: ANYONE WHO READS THE NEWS

India in May 2026 is not in crisis. But it is acting like a household that has decided to start saving before the rains come. The Prime Minister didn't ask citizens to sacrifice for the rupee directly — he asked them to walk to work, skip a vacation, and pass on a chain. In plain language: conserve dollars now, or pay more for everything later.

The setup will feel familiar to anyone who remembers 1991, when India almost ran out of dollars. Three of the four ingredients of that crisis are back in milder form — expensive oil, a widening import bill, and a falling rupee. The fourth — empty reserves — is not. India still has $602 billion in savings, about nine months of imports. That is why we are in a "defensive phase," not a real crisis.

→ NEXT 6 MONTHS
Tighten the Belt
Gold duty stays at 15%. Petrol and food prices keep rising slowly. Rupee drifts toward 97–98. RBI sells dollars now and then to slow the fall. Inflation stays in the 6.4–7.2% range. No interest rate cuts.
→ NEXT 12 MONTHS
A Real Choice
If oil falls below $90, this all eases. Gold duty comes back down, rupee strengthens, rate cuts return. If oil stays above $100, expect more government action — possibly new import restrictions or fresh borrowing from abroad in dollars.
→ NEXT 24 MONTHS
The Worst Case
A war or shutdown in the Middle East that takes oil to $140. Probability is low (under 8%), but if it happens, India would have to do emergency import controls, rates would shoot up, and the rupee could cross 100. Watch this corner carefully.

The most important thing the speech tells us is not that India is in trouble. It is that the government is acting before the data forces it to. That's the opposite of what India did in 1991, and it's why this is a defensive phase rather than a defensive failure. When a government changes citizen behavior ahead of the bad numbers, it's telling you what it expects the bad numbers to look like in three months.

Our best guess (50%): the current measures hold the line and the rupee slowly settles around 97. There's a 25% chance things actually get better in the next few months (the bullish case). There's a 20% chance things get worse before they get better, and a 5% chance of a real crisis. Those are the four roads. The speech was the government making sure we don't end up on the worst one.

Three things to watch in the next 60 days: (1) the next inflation print in June — does it cross 6.8%? (2) India's foreign currency reserves by end-June — are they still above $590 billion? (3) any announcement about cooking oil import duty being cut. The first two tell you whether the defense is working. The third tells you whether the government feels it has to throw a lifeline to households — a sign the political pressure is starting to bite.
